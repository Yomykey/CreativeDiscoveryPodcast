Setting up a business

Cambodia offers 100% foreign ownership in many sectors, but the registration process has its quirks. You'll need a local registered agent, a minimum of one Khmer shareholder for certain structures, and patience with the Ministry of Commerce. Most founders we've spoken to recommend starting with a sole proprietorship or LLC and upgrading as you scale.

The real advice from our guests: don't try to do it remotely. Be here, meet the lawyers in person, and understand that relationships matter more than paperwork.

Investing

Property is the most popular entry point for foreign investors — condos in Phnom Penh can still be acquired for a fraction of Bangkok or Ho Chi Minh City prices. The Cambodia Securities Exchange (CSX) is small but growing, and agriculture and logistics represent longer-term plays. Foreign ownership of land is restricted, but strata title condos and long-term leases are well-established routes.

The risk is real: due diligence is harder here than in developed markets, and the legal framework is evolving. Every investor we've interviewed says the same thing — work with local advisors you trust.

Money & banking

Cambodia runs on a dual-currency system — US dollars and Cambodian riel. Most business is conducted in USD, which simplifies things for foreigners. Opening a bank account is straightforward with a passport and business registration. International transfers work but can be slow; most expats use a combination of local banks and services like Wise.

Tax obligations are real and enforced more than they used to be. Get a local accountant early — it's cheap and saves headaches.

Living here

Cambodia offers one of the easiest visa regimes in Southeast Asia — a business visa is renewable indefinitely and costs around $300/year. Healthcare is improving but most expats in Phnom Penh and Siem Reap use private clinics or fly to Bangkok for serious procedures. International schools are available in both cities. Cost of living is low by Western standards but rising in central Phnom Penh.

Learning even basic Khmer opens doors. The expat community is tight-knit and genuinely helpful if you show up willing to engage.
