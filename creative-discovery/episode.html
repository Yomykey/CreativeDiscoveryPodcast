How We Built a $2M Property Portfolio in Phnom Penh

About this episode

Sarah Chen moved to Phnom Penh in 2016 with a laptop and a hunch. Eight years later she runs a property portfolio worth over two million dollars. In this episode she walks through exactly how she did it — the first deal that nearly went wrong, how foreign ownership actually works in Cambodia, and the neighbourhoods she's quietly buying in now.

We get into the numbers most people won't share publicly: financing, yields, the exit strategy, and the mistakes that cost her early on. If you've ever wondered whether Cambodian property is a real opportunity or a trap for outsiders, this is the conversation to start with.

Show full transcript

JB: Sarah, welcome to Creative Discovery. You've become something of a legend in the Phnom Penh property scene. But I want to go back to the beginning — what brought you here in the first place?

Sarah: Thanks for having me, Joachim. Honestly, it was kind of an accident. I was working in Singapore in finance, burned out, and a friend said "come visit me in Phnom Penh for a week." That week turned into a month. I just felt this energy — things were being built everywhere, and nobody really knew what the city was going to become.

JB: And you saw property as the opportunity?

Sarah: Not immediately. I freelanced for a few months first, got to know the city. But I kept noticing these buildings going up, and the prices were — by Singapore or Hong Kong standards — absurdly low. So I started asking questions. And honestly, the more I asked, the more I realised nobody had clean answers about how foreign ownership worked here. That's what pulled me in.

JB: Let's talk about that first deal. You've said publicly that it almost went very wrong.

Sarah: Yeah, that's the polite version. My first deal was a condo in BKK1 — a pre-sale, which sounded like a great idea at the time. I put down a deposit, signed what I thought was a standard contract, and then the developer just... stopped building. For eighteen months. No communication, no refund clause that I could actually enforce. I learned more about Cambodian contract law in those eighteen months than most lawyers know.

JB: How did you get out of it?

Sarah: Persistence and relationships. I found a local lawyer who actually understood the strata title system, negotiated an exit, and got most of my deposit back. But the lesson was clear: due diligence here isn't what it looks like in Singapore. You need boots on the ground, you need relationships, and you need to understand that the legal framework is still being built in real time.

JB: And that didn't scare you off — it made you go deeper.

Sarah: Exactly. Because once I understood the risks, I could see the opportunity more clearly. The fundamentals — young population, urbanisation, GDP growth, proximity to Vietnam and Thailand — those haven't changed. If anything, they've gotten stronger. The people who got burned were the ones who treated it like a Western market. I decided to treat it like what it is: an emerging market with enormous upside if you respect the local reality.
